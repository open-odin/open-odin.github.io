One reason elegant solutions are so satisfying is that they erase the evidence of how hard the problem was.

When something is solved well, it often looks inevitable in retrospect. Of course that was the right abstraction. Of course that was the cleanest sentence. Of course that was the right simplification. Once the answer is visible, it becomes strangely difficult to remember what made the question difficult.

I think this is why elegance gets confused with ease.

From the outside, elegant work can look like someone moved quickly and effortlessly toward the right shape. But usually the opposite is true. Elegance is often what remains after a lot of false starts, awkward drafts, extra parts, and wrong assumptions have been removed. It is not the absence of struggle. It is struggle that has been edited.

That matters because it changes how you interpret your own mess while you're in it. If the final form of good work is clean, the middle almost never is. The middle is where you overbuild, then notice. Where you explain too much, then cut. Where you solve the wrong problem first and only later understand why it felt wrong.

Maybe that's the real trick: not expecting clarity too early.

Elegant solutions seem obvious in hindsight because good solutions compress the path that produced them. They hide the branching, the uncertainty, the discarded versions. What you admire at the end is not just simplicity. It's simplicity that survived contact with the full complexity of the problem.
